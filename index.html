Fire Escape Development Journal

May 29, 2017

Fire Escape is my most complex Cardboard project yet. It's been over three months since I started working on it and I am on the third iteration of the house and elevator models, which are central to the game. The goal is simple: the player needs to rescue his pet guinea pigs from a burning house.

In Die Hard, there's an elevator shaft scene where a fireball almost engulfs Kevin Costner's character. So I thought, why not throw in some special effects and suspense into my VR capstone project? And off I went, spending a lot of time looking at elevator models online (because my Blender modeling skills are not there yet).

It turned out that the big challenge is to integrate the elevator into the mesh of a house. Here are screenshots of the first two attempts.

The model on the top was modified in SketchUp and imported into Unity. Unfortunately, it did not look good in Unity in several places and patching the mesh with a plane here and there made things worse.

After several weeks of after-hours work, I was quite happy with the second iteration that is on the bottom. There were a few awkward places around the elevator but those imperfections looked fine with the textures applied to the model.

I was getting pretty close to the end: the game logic was there, the animations and special effects were complete and the UI was mostly done, too. I wanted some in-depth design feedback before I wrapped up development and began the Apple and Google Play store submission process.

And... the universe finally gave me a break. The Udacity VR program is really my only venue for VR-related professional development -- not a lot of people know about VR in SC and there are certainly no startups (that I know of) engaged in VR application development.

A Udacity classmate mentioned this raffle thing for discounted Google IO 2017 tickets. I thought, yeah, that's a long shot but I entered anyway out of curiosity. The form said they'd charge me the price of the ticket if I won, blah blah.

This was in early April. I continued working on Fire Escape. And then lo and behold, I get an email saying congratulations, we've charged your credit card, and we'll see you in Mountain View in May.

The last time I was in Mountain View was way back in 2014 during the final phase of my co-op. I miss both the city and that general feeling of sunny optimism and opportunity that permeates Silicon Valley. Of course, it helped that internships did't feel like full-time work, so there was time to run off and just explore stuff.

Anyway, the IO schedule indicated several sessions about Daydream and Tango, the augmented reality platform, so I booked the plane tickets and flew West.

Huge thanks to Women Techmakers for the ticket!

Google didn't disappoint. The logistics involved in taking care of some 7,000 people at the event were impressive: there were contractors for transportation, food, security and general event organization. Multiple sessions on topics ranging from machine learning to marketing to IoT devices were scheduled every hour from early morning till after six.

There was only a small but persistent group of people interested in getting feedback about their VR/AR apps. I heard predictable bad news about releasing Fire Escape on the iPhone: 1) the GVR SDK wasn't optimized for the iPhone and 2) there was no indication that Daydream would be available on iPhones at all, ever.

My view on this as a developer with very limited financial and temporal resources is first of all, that I need to develop for VR with the tools that I have, not the tools that I'd like to have. Which means I am releasing this thing for the iPhone and for Cardboard, instead of waiting to get a Daydream-compatible device and the Daydream headset.

Second, I want to reach the entire mobile market. And yes, Android has the lion's share of it but that doesn't mean all those iPhone users should miss out on affordable VR experiences. I don't know that many people who would be willing to shell out ~ $700 for a high-end Android device if they already have iPhones.

I did get some feedback on Fire Escape: I need to fix the noticable lag that occurs in parts of the mesh and spruce up the UI.

Since returning, I've been trying to figure out what's slowing the application down. All the shaders are set to mobile, so I eventually narrowed it down to the house mesh itself and... jettisoned it with great sadness. Here's what the third iteration of the house-elevator environment looks like now.

This is a simpler mesh and I hope this will solve the lag issue. I'll have to redo the door animations and re-arrange the guinea pig models before I build to iPhone but the good news is that I notice no lag in Unity play mode.

VR Space

VR Space is a two-level game for Google Cardboard with two simple objectives: collecting space trash and shooting down asteroids. The game includes ambient sounds and explosion special effects. It was developed during Udacity VR Jam #1 with two other collaborators in February 2017.

VR Expo

VR Expo gives the player an opportunity to explore five booths that feature different industries that are very likely to use VR in the near future. All booths have audio and most have interactive elements.

The Puzzler

The Puzzler is an immersive mobile VR application that uses “Simon Says” game mechanic. The game is set in a medieval dungeon which sits in a remote mountain valley. As the player hears the plaintive sounds of Scottish bagpipes, she is invited to enter the dungeon and solve the puzzle.
